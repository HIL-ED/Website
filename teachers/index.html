Our Methodology: Experiential Narrative Education

Every game we build applies Experiential Narrative Education (ENE), a framework where teachers and students co-create a narrative that frames learning as an active, story-driven experience. Students engage with real academic content through roleplay, choice, challenge, and consequence, and a deliberate debrief turns the experience into something they can name, connect to the curriculum, and remember. ENE adds no new content to your class and requires no shift in curriculum; it reframes the teaching you already do. Read the full framework →

What It Looks Like: One Class Period with Passed

Here's how a single 50-minute civics period runs with our policy simulation Passed, which targets the standard "opportunities for civic engagement within the structures of government are made possible through political and public policy processes."

1

Set up · 10 minutes

Students open the game in their browser, no login required, and each choose a policy (healthcare, minimum wage, education funding…) and a starting position, from grassroots organizer to member of Congress. Each choice comes with trade-offs they'll have to own.

2

Play · 25 minutes

Students grow public support and push their bill from public opinion to city hall, the statehouse, and Congress, spending influence points on canvassing, lobbying, coalition-building, and testimony while organized opposition pushes back.

3

Debrief · 15 minutes

Using the printable discussion sheet, students compare runs: Which action type felt most powerful? Would a different starting role have needed a different strategy? What real-world systems did your campaign resemble? The reflection is the assessment: that's where the learning gets named.
