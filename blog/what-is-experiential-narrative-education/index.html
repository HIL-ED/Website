Teaching Philosophy

What Is Experiential Narrative Education (and Why We Build Games Around It)

Picture a traditional social studies classroom: a teacher at the front, students half-watching the clock, a worksheet waiting at the end of the period. It's not that lecture has no place. It's that on its own, it rarely sticks. Chronic absenteeism is up since the pandemic, attention is more fragmented than ever, and the old formula of "read the chapter, take the quiz" is losing ground against a world of infinite competing distractions.

That's the problem we designed every one of our games to solve. We call the framework behind them Experiential Narrative Education, or ENE.

The Short Version

ENE is an educational framework where teachers and students co-create a narrative that frames learning as an active, story-driven experience. The narrative stays tied to real academic content, but students engage with it through roleplay, choice, challenge, and consequence, not just notes and recall.

"ENE is not adding new content to your class. It requires no shift in curriculum. It's a shift in mindset: taking the teaching you already do and framing it through narrative and experience."

The Four Pillars

Student Ownership

Students make real decisions inside the narrative instead of just observing it.

Narrative Framing

Content is wrapped in a story (a role, a setting, a stake) instead of delivered flat.

Interactive Structure

Rules, roles, and consequences give the narrative shape and keep it moving.

Reflective Assessment

A deliberate debrief turns the experience into something students can name and remember.

Short, Medium, and Long-Form Narratives

Not every ENE experience needs to take over your semester. A short-form narrative might run a single class period: students spend twenty minutes identifying hidden Red Scare informants. A medium-form narrative might stretch over a week or two, like watching a plague slowly spread across a simulated Europe. A long-form narrative, the kind our full games are built for, can run an entire unit, with students returning to the same roles and consequences again and again as the story compounds.

The length isn't the point. The point is that the narrative runs alongside the content you're already required to teach, not instead of it.

Why It Works

Roleplaying and simulation are well-studied tools in experiential learning, shown to help with short-term memory retention, engagement, confidence in the subject matter, and what researchers call historical consciousness: the sense that the past was shaped by real decisions, made by people who didn't know how the story would end. That's exactly the gap between "memorizing that the Constitution was ratified in 1788" and actually feeling the tension of the ratification debates.

Every game on this site, from lobbying a bill through Congress to rebuilding a city through the Industrial Revolution, is an attempt to put a student inside that tension instead of just describing it to them.
