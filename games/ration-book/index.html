A Homefront Simulation

Ration Book

You don't run this war from the front. You run it from the kitchen table: stretching ration stamps, keeping a household fed and hopeful, and living with the draft's knock on the door. Every choice you make was made by a real American family, in World War I or World War II.

The goal

Keep your household's Health, Morale, and standing in the community (Trust) as high as you can across the whole war, while managing scarce cash. There's no single "win" state: the point is to feel the trade-offs the homefront actually forced on families, and to see how your choices add up by the end.

The Ration Board

Each turn, your household needs a set of goods: food, fuel, and other essentials that were tightly rationed during both world wars. For each one, you'll choose:

  • Go Without — free, but your family's Health and Morale take the full hit of the shortage.
  • Ration Fairly — use your legal stamp allotment and modest cash. This is what the government asked of everyone. It may not fully cover your need once shortages deepen, but it costs you the least in community standing.
  • Buy on the Black Market — pay a steep cash premium to get everything you need, no stamps required. It works, but it damages your Trust in the community, and you risk getting caught.

War Bonds

Each turn you can put spare cash into War Bonds. You won't see that money again until after the war, but it builds Morale and Trust: it's how ordinary families helped finance the war effort.

The Draft, and the rest of the household

Your household includes a spouse and a draft-age son. As the war goes on, you'll face the draft lottery, letters from the front, and a scripted arc where your spouse takes on new work outside the home. These moments carry their own choices and consequences.

Random events

At the start of each turn, a newspaper shows you what's happening nationally. Some turns bring a specific event you'll need to respond to. Not everything is in your control: that's the point.

At the end

You'll get a debrief on how your household fared, and you can print a full record of your choices along with reflection questions for class discussion.
