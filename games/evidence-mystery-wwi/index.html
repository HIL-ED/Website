Case Briefing

International Historical Commission — Case File WWI-1914

Your Assignment

You have been retained by an international historical commission, ninety years after the guns fell silent, to help settle one of history's most argued-over questions: who — or what — caused the First World War?

You will not find a single confession, a single villain, or a single correct answer. Instead you will examine 28 primary and secondary sources — telegrams, treaties, mobilization plans, cartoons, posters, newspapers, and diaries — evaluate each one as a real historian would, and build your own evidence-based argument about which causes mattered most. Two careful investigators can examine the same evidence and reach different, equally defensible conclusions. That is not a flaw in this case file. That is how history actually works.

What You Will Practice

  • Sourcing — who made this, and why?
  • Corroboration — do other sources agree?
  • Contextualization — what was happening around this document?
  • Evaluating reliability and recognizing bias
  • Weighing long-term causes against a single triggering event
  • Building a claim-evidence-reasoning argument a historian would respect

Eight Categories of Cause

Every piece of evidence in this case relates to one or more of these categories. Keep them in mind as you investigate — you will eventually have to rank them.

How the Investigation Works

  1. Open files in the Evidence Locker. Read each source, analyze who made it and why, rate its importance and reliability, tag it, and take notes.
  2. Pin examined evidence to the Case Board and connect it to the cause(s) you believe it supports.
  3. Rank the eight causes from most to least significant, in your own judgment.
  4. Build your argument: a claim, supporting evidence, reasoning, a counterargument, and a rebuttal.
  5. Receive your Investigation Rating and reflect on what changed your mind.
